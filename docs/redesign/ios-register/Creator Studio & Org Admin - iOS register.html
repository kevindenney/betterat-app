BetterAt redesign · Roles, role surfaces & the org plane

The roles a person can wear, the surfaces those roles open, and how an institution gets here in the first place

The last few passes designed the practitioner surfaces — where Felix and Emily do the work. Commentary · what this pass commits to

The seams between roles are designed; the seams between products aren't there.

Five small commitments that determine whether the org plane feels like the same app or like a different one.

One avatar, every role.

The profile-button dropdown is the only role-switcher in the app. It appears identically in the top-right corner of every screen — Practice, Library, Discover, Reflect. The contents adapt to who's looking at it, but the affordance never moves. Karen Murphy authors a blueprint, mentors a cohort, and tracks her own boxing practice from the same avatar.

The alternative considered: a "Workspace" tab in the bottom nav. Rejected — adding a fifth tab to support roles 90% of users don't have would be a tax on the 90% who don't. The dropdown is invisible until you need it.

Creator Studio and Org admin are siblings, not children.

They share a shell — sidebar with org card, ctx-switch pill, nav rail, user card; main with crumbs, h1, sub-h1, studio-tabs. They differ only in (a) accent color (purple vs. navy), (b) the items in the rail, and (c) what's allowed in the main pane.

This is deliberate. A Hopkins faculty member who's also a Hopkins admin should feel one click apart from her two surfaces, not two products apart. The component vocabulary persists.

The marketing site bridges directly into the admin console.

Frame 11 ends with "open admin" as step 4. There is no separate "welcome to BetterAt" screen between checkout and the admin's first day. Dean Park lands on Frame 7 with a 30-day trial banner, 350 reserved seats, and a verified domain. The continuity earns the price tag.

Institutional authors don't see payouts. Independent authors do.

Frame 5 (Murphy at Hopkins) shows a navy info card in the pricing section that explicitly disables payouts and points to the author terms with Hopkins. Frame 6 (Noor in Berlin) shows the full payouts surface — chart, breakdown by blueprint, Stripe Connect, SEPA. Same Studio shell, different drawer contents, gated by which org the author is currently signed in to.

The reason this matters: an institutional author writing under a faculty agreement should never accidentally collect a personal payment. The chrome enforces the contract.

Org context is a chip in the header, never a chrome theme.

Frame 14 (Emily, post-onboarding) shows the JH chip living next to the interest name in the top header — quiet, navy, the same place she'll see it forever. The chrome's accent color does not change to navy for Hopkins students; that would be performance. The chip is enough.
