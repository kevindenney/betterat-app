The creator surface — where blueprints are written, published, and watched

This is Surface 3 of the Five Surfaces addendum: the desktop console a creator uses to author blueprints, manage subscribers, and read the analytics that come back. The creator on screen is Bram van Olphen, a Dragon-class sailing coach whose flagship blueprint is Dragon Worlds Prep — a 14-step program with 28 paying subscribers.

The creator surface inherits the same product chrome as Org Admin: black BetterAt mark, sidebar product nav, white window on a warm review ground. iOS blue still owns actions; SF Pro still owns UI. What changes is the strap — instead of an institutional shield it shows the creator’s own avatar and the words Creator Dashboard. The tools are different; the gravity isn’t.

Frame 2 · Editor

Editor · metadata above, fourteen drag-handled steps, publishing on the rail

The tab strip is replaced by a focused editor toolbar — back, title, autosave status, Preview, Save. Steps are a vertical list with explicit numbers and grip handles; the rounded indices stay visible so reorder doesn’t feel destructive. Step 6 — Mark roundings carries a stuck point flag, the same signal that lights up on Insights; it’s editorial guidance, not a system error, so the styling is amber, not coral. The right rail keeps the heavy decisions — publish, price, and a quick glance at who’s on the blueprint right now.

Frame 4 · Insights

Insights · the dashboard that argues for a specific edit

The heatmap is the centerpiece — fourteen horizontal bars, one per step, with the two amber outliers calling out exactly which steps are weak. Step 6 (Mark roundings) is the stuck point the spec calls out: only 47% finish it and the average time on step is 8.2 days against a 4.8-day baseline, which is what the Average Time per Step chart underneath corroborates. The stall callout on the right rail reads like advice, not a report: the headline is the fix, not the problem; the button takes you straight back into the editor at Step 6.

Mobile-responsive degradation exists — the sidebar collapses into a sheet, the tab strip becomes a select, and the editor switches to a single-column scroll with the publishing rail pushed to the bottom — but the canonical view is desktop. The creator surface is where work gets done; the consumer-facing iOS app is where blueprints are read.

What the creator surface inherits from Org Admin

Same product chrome: black BetterAt mark, sidebar product nav, white window on the warm review ground. SF Pro for UI; iOS blue is still the single owner of action color. The strap structure is identical — a 56px row above the tab strip — but instead of an institutional shield it carries the creator’s own avatar disc and the words Creator Dashboard.

This is deliberate. A creator who also administers an organization should never feel like she switched products when she moves between the two surfaces. The grammar is shared; the verbs differ.

Cover placeholders, not boats

Each blueprint card has a cover slot, but we draw a striped placeholder with a monospace tag rather than a fake illustration. A real creator uploads their own image; until then a card carries a flat color block in the program’s key palette and a typeset title spine. The draft uses a dashed-grey placeholder — visually distinct, structurally identical to a published cover.

This is the default-aesthetic rule applied to the creator product: never hand-draw a sailboat in SVG when the real cover is one upload away.

The stuck-point flag

Step 6 carries an amber stuck point badge in three places: in the editor steps list, in the heatmap on Insights, and inside the stall-card callout. Same shape, same color, same wording in all three. The flag is editorial, not an error — amber, not coral — and never appears for a step until at least 15% of subscribers have stalled there.

The whole point of the metric is to argue for an edit. Hence the stall callout’s button goes back into the editor at the exact step, not to a separate analytics page.

What got skipped

Earnings is shown as a tab and a sidebar nav item but not drawn; the flow is standard Stripe Connect (creator-balance, next-payout, transactions list, tax-info) and the visual treatment is the same widget grammar used elsewhere. Settings is out of scope for this addendum.

The + New Blueprint button opens the AI Coach scaffold — the empty-state flow lives in a separate canonical. From this screen it’s just a button.
