Updated for the Pass N rename. The Library tab replaces the Playbook tab; the term concepts replaces playbook entries. These four phones now live inside the Library tab rather than under Discover. Phone 1 is now reached via Library → Sources scope, not Discover → Library. The content survives; the destination clarifies.

The Library was lost somewhere between passes. It comes back here as a first-class surface, not a sub-tab afterthought. Four phones, one architecture. A nursing student in the MSN program at Johns Hopkins carries a vast working library — protocols, practice alerts, textbook chapters, journal articles, bedside videos, recorded lectures, podcast episodes, her own scanned notes — and every step she runs draws from it. The Library is the catalog. Steps and concepts cite it. Concepts are born from it — the resource is the prime source of the playbook idea. Capture is one tap from anywhere.

Phone 1 is the catalog home (Library tab · Sources scope). Phone 2 is a single library item showing the back-references — concepts that cite it, steps that include it, what Emily marked. Phone 4

Quick capture · one tap from anywhere

Four modes (link / upload / photo / paste) under a coral-sparkle sheet. After capture, the system detects format and topic, suggests a collection, and offers an optional attachment to a concept or step. The same sheet is reachable from the Library home "Drop something in" card and from any step's "Add from library" row — the destination is the difference, the sheet is the same.

What this pass commits to

The Library is a first-class surface, lives as its own tab, and is the prime source of playbook concepts. (Updated for the Pass N rename — this pass originally proposed Library as a sub-tab of Discover; the rename promoted it to a peer of Discover instead.)

The architectural mistake earlier was treating the playbook as if it were the library. D28 was right — the playbook is the user's conceptual home, not a resource library — but the necessary other half got dropped: there has to be an actual library. A nursing student at Johns Hopkins isn't carrying around a small handful of concepts; she's carrying a working library of protocols, alerts, chapters, articles, videos, recorded lectures, scanned notes, and podcast episodes, and her concepts are the synthesis layer on top of that catalog.

The Pass N rename made this clean: Library replaces Playbook as the tab name; concepts replaces playbook entries as the term for the synthesis layer; Sources and Concepts live as siblings under Library. The four-tab root nav becomes Race / Library / Discover / Reflect — your stuff sits in Library, other people's stuff sits in Discover.

Resources are prime sources of concepts. The back-reference is the proof.

Phone 2's Where this appears in your practice block is the load-bearing surface for the whole architecture. A single PDF shows:

  • Originconcept that was seeded by a marked phrase in this resource. The italic-serif quote on the row is the phrase itself — the same italic-serif-with-provenance grammar locked on Race Prep, scaled down here to a list-row label.
  • Citedconcept that references this resource in its trail but didn't originate from it.
  • In stepstep that included this resource as read before shift or pinned inside a beat.

This three-row taxonomy is the whole vocabulary of how a library item connects to the playbook system. Origin is the strongest tie, cited is the secondary tie, in-step is the operational tie. Future surfaces (a concept detail showing all its origin and cited resources, a step archive showing every resource pulled in) just invert this same relation.

A step has two library hooks: before and during.

Before the shift — a checklist of resources Emily commits to bring with her into the shift. Read, watch, or skim, with circle-checks she ticks off; the system can suggest items based on the shift's clinical context, Patricia's recommendations, and Emily's open concepts. Same component grammar as a Race Prep beat list, with library-format glyphs replacing the toolbar icons.

During the shift — pinned references inside individual beats. A Bates' chapter callout sits inside Assessment round, a JHH Code Blue pocket card sits inside Charting & flags. The reference is the exact passage Emily anticipates needing at that moment — not "go look this up," but "this is the answer, already brought to where you'll need it."

Both hooks are composed during step authoring: when Patricia (path author) builds the step, she pulls from her own library to scaffold what students should bring. When Emily runs the step, she can add her own. The same grammar works in both directions.

Capture is one tap, four modes, three destinations.

The capture sheet is the gesture that makes the library compound — if adding takes effort, the library starves. Four input modes cover the realistic surfaces a nursing student encounters:

  • Linkarticle, video, podcast episode, web protocol. URL field, fetcher does the rest.
  • UploadPDF (the dominant case in clinical), EPUB, audio file, video file. Drop-zone with format auto-detection.
  • Photoscan a textbook page, a printed protocol, a whiteboard from a lecture. OCR runs server-side; image becomes a searchable note with the photo embedded.
  • Pastefor paywalled articles she's already pulled, lecture transcripts, copied passages from a closed book.

After capture, the system detects — format, topic tags, source, year — using the existing coral-sparkle "something's about to be made" mark. The auto-meta block is editable; nothing is fixed without confirmation. Then the three destinations: Standalone (default — goes to library), Concept (also attaches as a starting source for an existing or new concept), Step (also adds to the current step's before the shift list or a beat reference).

The sheet is the same in all three call sites — Library home's Drop something in, a step's Add from library row, a concept's Add a source affordance. The destination is the difference. The capture is the constant.

Format-typed accents disambiguate without becoming signal colors.

Six format hues. Each used only on library-item chrome — the spine on a shelf card, the source-glyph background on a recent-row, the chip in a step's prep list, the strip on a collection card. None of them carries iOS-blue's action meaning or coral's system-attention meaning. They are identity, not signal.

PDF · brick Video · aubergine Book · ink blue Link · slate Audio · teal Note · ochre

The two locked signal colors stay in their lanes: coral · system-attention iOS blue · action

If a format card needs to indicate "active in a step right now," it gets the coral live-dot — same component, same grammar as Playbook's concept cards. Format hue and signal color coexist on the card without conflict because they describe different axes: what kind of thing this is versus what the system is paying attention to.

Collections, not folders.

The bottom of the Library home offers Collections — named topical bundles (Sepsis & rapid response, Cardiac & telemetry, Pediatric vitals & meds, High-alert pharmacology). Distinguished from folders: an item lives in the catalog regardless; collections are tags surfaced as cards. An item can belong to multiple collections. The little color strip on each collection card previews the format mix inside — quick read of "this collection is mostly PDFs and one video" versus "mostly book chapters and notes."

Collections are AI-suggested on capture (the sheet's Collection row) and user-editable. Patricia's authored paths can ship with a recommended collection of starting resources — "Subscribe to this path; this collection joins your library."

Architectural commitments preserved.

  • Four-tab navPre-Clin · Library (active on Phones 1, 2, 4) · Discover · Reflect. Library is now its own tab — promoted from sub-tab to peer in Pass N. The root nav is otherwise unchanged.
  • Per-interest vocabularyPre-Clinical / On Shift / Debrief for nursing; the four-tab analogues swap as before.
  • AI never speaks as itselfthe From your library coral eyebrow on Phone 3 is the same component as From your playbook on Race Prep iOS — system surfacing user's own material.
  • Raw / synthesized layer separationthe resource is raw, what Emily marked in it is raw, the concept that grew from a marked phrase is synthesized. Resynthesize from any source is preserved.
  • Italic serif for considered prose with attributionthe marked excerpts on Phone 2, the seeded phrase on Phone 2's origin row, the AI offer body on Phone 3 — all in italic serif because they are user-marked prose with attribution. The locked grammar holds.
  • No fifth tab, no “Learn” tabLibrary is now a peer of Discover, not a sub-tab. The root nav remains four-up.
  • No gamification, no streaks, no progress metersthe prep-list circle-checks are not progress, they are commitment-and-confirmation: Emily ticks an item after she has actually read it.

Named absences.

  • No global search-first homethe Library home opens to the catalog, not a search bar. Search lives in the top-right glyph. Curation by what's in play this week and recently added is what most users return for; full-text search is the secondary action.
  • No folder treecollections are tags surfaced as cards, not a hierarchy. An item is one place: in the catalog. It surfaces in multiple collections.
  • No standalone "Read it later" inboxcaptures land directly in the catalog, optionally attached. An unread item is just an item whose read-checkmark hasn't been ticked — not a separate queue.
  • No social signal on Library itemspeer activity is a Discover → Paths signal, not a Library signal. The library is Emily's catalog, not a feed of what her cohort is reading.
  • No publishing affordance on Phone 2resources Emily has authored herself (her own typed notes, photo-captured scans) can be shared, but the act of preparing-to-share is a separate composition surface, in keeping with the same discipline as Trophy v2.

The test for this pass.

Two questions. First: does the library feel necessary, not just nice? The bet is that the Phone-2 back-reference block earns the surface — if a resource showing its three roles in Emily's practice reads as "of course this is here," the architectural argument is sound. If it reads as "useful but optional," the integration into Steps and Concepts isn't deep enough yet.

Second: does it hold the locked register? The chrome is the same as the prior twelve iOS surfaces — gray 6 ground, 16px-radius white cards, SF Pro throughout, iOS blue for action, iOS coral for system-attention, italic-serif for considered prose with attribution. The only new vocabulary is the six format-typed hues, scoped strictly to library-item chrome. If it reads as continuous with Playbook, Race Prep, and Competency Assessment, the register holds.
